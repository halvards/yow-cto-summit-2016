APRA

Australian Prudential Regulation Authority

The regulator of the Australian financial services industry, established July 1998.
Oversees banks, credit unions, building societies, insurance, superannuation.
Ensure that financial promises made by institutions can be met.
By some, credited with helping Australia avoid the worst of the GFC.

Myth

Offshoring

“Using a cloud provider that is incorporated in another country is considered offshoring by APRA regardless of where the data centre is located.”
This would cover most public cloud providers.
APRA has strict reporting standards for offshoring.

The US Patriot Act often gets a mention, can they access the data?
It's true that legal jurisdiction can get complicated, and this should be considered.

Sources of myth

  1. Prudential Practice Guide CPG 235 - Managing Data Risk:
    Six of eight mentions of "outsource" is written as “outsource/offshore”

  2. 2010 APRA letter to regulated entities contained the subject line “Outsourcing and offshoring - Specific considerations when using cloud computing services'
Confuses outsourcing with offshoring.

Gives the impression that public cloud is automatically offshoring.

Early on, most public cloud didn't have data centres in Australia.

Evidence against myth

APRA Prudential Standard CPS 231 - Outsourcing:

“Offshoring includes arrangements where the service provider is incorporated in Australia, but the physical location of the outsourced activity is outside Australia.”
“Offshoring does not include arrangements where the physical location of an outsourced activity is within Australia but the service provider is not incorporated in Australia.”
So it's all about the physical location of the data centre.

Offshoring is confused with outsourcing.

Myth

Business continuity

“APRA's prudential standards says we must be ready to redeploy applications in-house at any time in case of a significant disruption at the cloud provider.”
Implication: We must have full redundancy on internal hardware, which will be too expensive.

Source of myth

APRA Prudential Standard CPS 231: Outsourcing

“An APRA-regulated institution must be able to demonstrate to APRA that [...] it has taken into account [...] contingency issues in accordance with Prudential Standard CPS 232 Business Continuity Management should the outsourced activity need to be brought in-house”
"An APRA-regulated institution must maintain at all times a documented BCP [business continuity plan]”
"Where material business activities are outsourced, an APRA-regulated institution must satisfy itself as to the adequacy of the outsourced service providerâ€™s BCP"

Evidence against myth [1]

APRA Information Paper: Outsourcing involving shared computing services (including cloud)

“[...] under CPS 231 and SPS 231, APRA-regulated entities must develop contingency plans that allow for the shared computing service to be transitioned to an alternative service provider (or brought in-house), if required.”
Using an alternative service provider is an option.

'If required' - is the application critical to business continuity?

Evidence against myth [2]

No mention of "in-house" in CPS 232

“An APRA-regulated institution must maintain at all times a documented BCP [business continuity plan]”
“Where material business activities are outsourced, an APRA-regulated institution must satisfy itself as to the adequacy of the outsourced service provider's BCP”

Demonstrate Understanding

From 2010 letter on 'Outsourcing and offshoring - Specific considerations when using cloud computing services':

“In APRA's view, both materiality and risk assessments necessitate a detailed understanding of the [...] business processes [..], the technology architecture and the sensitive information [...] impacted by the outsourcing arrangement. APRA has observed that, to date, assessments of cloud computing proposals typically lack sufficient consideration of these factors.”
2015 Information paper: "Observed weaknesses":

Proposals driven purely by cost considerations rather than strategy and architectural roadmap.

Business cases that focus only on benefits and don't provide visibility of risks.
Public cloud considered outsourcing.
Entities must consult with APRA prior to entering into outsourcing arrangements where offshoring is involved.
APRA "questions the appropriateness of transitioning systems of record to a public cloud environment."

APP

Australian Privacy Principles

Outline how Australian Government agencies, private sector and not-for-profit organisations with an annual turnover of more than $3M, all private health service providers and some small businesses ('APP entities') must manage personal information.

Myth

“The Australian Privacy Principles mean that personal information cannot touch servers outside Australia without first obtaining the person's consent.”
Privacy is complicated!
Amends Privacy Act 1988. Replaced the National PP and Information PP in March 2014.
Applies to medium and large enterprises as well as government agencies.

Source of myth

APP 8: Cross-border disclosure of personal information

“8.1: Before an APP entity discloses personal information [...] the entity must take such steps [...] to ensure that the overseas recipient does not breach the Australian Privacy Principles”
“8.2: Subclause 8.1 does not apply [...] if [...] the entity expressly informs the individual [...] [and] the individual consents to the disclosure”
So disclosure to an overseas recipient requires informing the individual and getting consent.

"Use" or "disclose"?

APP 6 guidelines: Use or disclosure of personal information

“An APP entity 'uses' information where it handles [...] the information, within the entity's effective control.”
“An APP entity 'discloses' personal information where it makes it accessible to others outside the entity and releases the subsequent handling of the information from its effective control.”
“[...] the distinction is relevant to [...] the disclosure of personal information to an overseas recipient.”
If data passes through servers outside Australia, but it's encrypted properly, is this disclose? Email example.

Duty to handle information carefully.

'Unauthorised access' is also different.

ASD

Australian Signals Directorate

Certifies cloud providers for government use.
Provides security accreditation for government systems.
Publishes the Australian Government Information Security Manual, which governs the security of government IT systems.
Publishes Cloud Computing Security advice.
Part of Department of Defence.
This isn't all they do, it's just what's most relevant to this talk.

Myth

Multi-tenancy and virtualisation

“The Australian Signals Directorate (ASD) does not allow government agencies and departments to use shared multi-tenant hosting due to the risk of breach from a compromised tenant.”
ASD is part of the Department of Defence. You'll know about this if you work in government.

Source of myth

ASD Cloud Computing Security Considerations (Sept 2012)

“What assurance do I have that the virtualisation [...] mechanisms guarantee adequate logical and network segregation between multiple tenants [...]?”
“For Infrastructure as a Service, the virtualisation software [...] was typically not originally designed to provide segregation for security purposes.”
ASD considers this document superceeded by their newer advice. It's still on their website because "some readers might find its conversational format useful to start a discussion"

Virtualisation

From ASD's Cloud Computing Security for Tenants:

Risk: “Tenant's data compromised by another malicious/compromised tenant”

Mitigation: “Use multi-tenancy mechanisms provided by the CSP [cloud service provider] e.g. to separate the tenant's web application and network traffic from other tenants”

With 10 footnotes just on this risk!
Half of the foonotes arelinks showing fixed security vulnerabilities in VMware, Hyper-V, XEN, KVM
The other half: links to reported security vulnerabilities in Docker.
Very useful list of risks and mitigations of using cloud IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS.

Interesting quote

From ASD's Cloud Computing Security for Tenants:

“Organisations need to perform a risk assessment and implement associated mitigations before using cloud services. [...] Organisations need to compare these risks against an objective risk assessment of using inhouse computer systems which might: be poorly secured; have inadequate availability; or, be unable to meet modern business requirements.”
Are your existing practices up to scratch?

If you're just getting started with considering public cloud risks and mitigations, the guidance from ASD is an excellent starting point.

Read the policies yourself!

Most of what you hear is interpretations of policies.

Interpretations are often biased, and based on information that may be out of date.
Maybe you can come up with another interpretation?

In the end, most regulation is good advice. As a private person I'm glad organisations are forced to take security and privacy seriously.
